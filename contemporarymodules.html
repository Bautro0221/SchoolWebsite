Lesson1: Arts in the Philippines

The development of Philippine arts is shaped by three major traditions: the Ethnic Tradition, the Spanish Colonial Tradition, and the American Colonial and Contemporary Arts Traditions. Even before colonization, the Philippines had its indigenous arts influenced by the geographical location of its ancestors. The arrival of the Spaniards replaced some of these indigenous arts with Western influences. During the Spanish rule, art forms were influenced by religion and secularization. After the Spanish era, the Americans introduced modern art during their rule. This led to the continuous development of art through urbanization, consumerism, and new technology, which shaped contemporary art.

Ethnic Tradition

Art forms in this period were influenced by geographical location and the everyday lives of the Filipinos. Arts reflected pagan beliefs, daily activities such as fishing and farming, and specific decorative patterns in communities.

  • Angono Petroglyphs: Located in Binangonan, Rizal, these petroglyphs were discovered by National Artist Carlos "Botong" Francisco in 1965. The 127 human and animal figures are dated to the late Neolithic period.
  • Manunggul Jar: A burial jar found in Manunggul Cave, Palawan, dating back to the early 1960s, signifying early Filipino beliefs in life after death.
  • Banga Dance: A traditional dance of the Kalinga tribe from the Mountain Province, showcasing the strength and grace of Igorot women.

Spanish Colonial Tradition

During the Spanish colonial era, art was used to propagate the Catholic faith and was heavily influenced by religion and secularization.

  • Langit, Lupa, at Impyerno by Josef Luciano Dans: A wall painting in St. James the Apostle Church in Paete, Laguna, depicting heaven, earth, and hell.
  • St. Paul Metropolitan Cathedral: Built in 1574 and rebuilt in 1641, this cathedral in Vigan City follows a Baroque architectural style.

American Colonial and Contemporary Arts Traditions

Modern Art

Modern art refers to the period between the 1860s and 1960s, with a focus on national identity.

  • The Making of the Philippine Flag by Fernando Amorsolo: A painting by Fernando Amorsolo showcasing the creation of the Philippine flag.
  • Bayanihan by Carlos “Botong” Francisco: A mural depicting the Filipino tradition of bayanihan, painted by Carlos Francisco in 1962.

Contemporary Art

Contemporary art reflects modern culture and society, emerging since the 1970s with diverse practices.

  • Cinco Marias (Tres Marias Series) by Federico Aguilar Alcuaz: A painting depicting various domestic activities.
  • Art Installation in Sand Dunes of Paoay by Leeroy New: A modern art installation symbolizing festivals and celebrations, created for the La Milagrosa Festival in Ilocos Norte.

Elements of Art

  • Space: Refers to the empty area in art, either positive (enclosed) or negative (surrounding the enclosed area).
  • Line: A series of connected points with various forms (curved, dotted, broken) and directions (vertical, horizontal, diagonal).
  • Shape and Form: Shape is two-dimensional with height and width, while form is three-dimensional with depth.
  • Color: Related to natural phenomena and light passing through a prism to create different hues.
  • Value: Refers to the lightness or darkness of a color.
  • Texture: Pertains to the feel or surface quality of an artwork, such as rough or smooth.

Principles of Art

  • Proportion: The relationship in size between elements in an artwork.
  • Harmony: The unity and arrangement of elements in an artwork.
  • Variety: Adding different elements to create interest in an artwork.
  • Movement: The illusion of motion in art.
  • Rhythm: Repetition of elements to create a flow or pattern.
  • Balance: The equal distribution of elements in an artwork.
  • Emphasis: The focal point or greater impact placed on certain elements.

Lesson 2: Art: Meaning and Forms

What is It?

The pictures presented in the previous activity are depictions of the artworks that are seen today. Present-day art has a lot of variety. There are various expressions of Philippine art that can be found right inside our homes and are found everywhere. The arrangement of furniture, home décor, the way a mother cooks a particular kind of food, jeepney designs and signages, architecture of buildings, billboard designs, and other unnoticed works of art comprise the “art of today.”

The artworks and styles of the past have evolved and adapted to a dynamic changing world, a world immersed in the latest technology and emerging ideologies. These artworks and styles are created, witnessed, and experienced in our lifetime by artists and style experts catering to Millennial and Gen Z viewers and tech-savvy clientele. These artworks are collectively known as Contemporary Art.

The term contemporary has been defined as something “happening, existing, living, or coming into being during the same period of time” (Merriam-Webster). Assuming this definition, Contemporary Art refers to art of any form and genre that is produced in our contemporary time, simply put as “art created today.” It should not be confused with Modern Art, which ranges from the end of the 19th Century until the 1970s or 1980s or the late 20th Century. This places Contemporary Art from the late 20th Century to the present. Modern Art differs from Contemporary Art historically and contextually.

Contemporary Art

Contemporary Art is influenced by the economic, social, political, and environmental context where the artist is immersed in. It can be said that Contemporary Art is the artist’s expression of his perception and comprehension of these contexts. Contemporary Arts in the Philippines is as diverse as our people. Contemporary Philippine Art is not confined to the usual genres of the four main art forms, namely, visual, literary, applied, and performing arts. Genres are categories of artistic creation and are characterized by similarities in form, style, or subject matter. Many other genres came up, which include pure art forms as well as mixed art forms.

Elements of Contemporary Art

Contemporary Arts have different elements that are put together to create a distinct artwork. These elements include:

  • Appropriation: This relates to the modifications made by the artist.
  • Hybridity: This refers to the use of different art mediums that are put together.
  • Technology: Its application highlights the creativity of the artist.
  • Performance: Carried out for viewers to experience.
  • Space: Intricate use of space.

These elements are combined in different ways to come up with Integrative Art. Integrative Art employs the use of different art elements used together in distinct expressions. Thus, it becomes a combination of different art forms for specific groups of viewers. Integrative art is also a fusion of different art disciplines using different styles and media in creating art. It can be found in different places of the country, usually in urban areas where artists have ready access to different art forms and media. Examples of Integrative Art include the use of computer applications combined with other media and the use of non-traditional art materials in creating artworks usually done with mixed art forms.

Contemporary Art Forms from the Philippine Regions

With modern technology and easy access to far-flung areas of the country, many artworks have become known, and many more are being created by present-day artists from the different regions. All forms of art can be found throughout the country. Artworks coming from the seventeen regions of the three main island groups of Luzon, Visayas, and Mindanao have been exhibited in different exhibition halls and museums. Viewers have come to know and, more importantly, understand the present-day art expressions of artists who want their culture, situation, and issues to be known through their artworks.

Distinct Features of Contemporary Philippine Art

  • Communal nature: Reflects the relationships built around the bonds of community and society.
  • Intuitive: Filipinos are immersed in their consciousness of themselves, their community, and their culture.
  • Holistic: Individuals, groups of people, and events are perceived in their entirety, as well as the unitive use of art materials and forms.
  • Creative process: The artist puts more emphasis on how their artwork was made than on the artwork itself.
  • Integrative: It encompasses religious, moral, spiritual, social, and environmental concerns.
  • Multi-focal: Considers everything and everyone, the wide perspective of the artwork as well as both artist and viewer.

What is It

The Philippine Contemporary Art can be classified into seven major art forms. These are painting, sculpture, architecture, film, literature, music and theater, and dance. Knowing these classifications will guide us in identifying and exploring different contemporary art forms from the regions of the Philippines. So, let’s start classifying and exploring the Philippine’s contemporary art forms!

PHILIPPINE CONTEMPORARY ARTS

Philippine contemporary arts can be described into visual arts, literary arts, and performing arts. Visual art is an expression of artistic ideas through images, structures, and tactile work. Some visual artworks are integrated, meaning it combines several mediums to create new and unique artwork. Painting, sculpture, architecture, and film are examples of visual arts.

Painting

Painting is the expression of ideas and emotions through the creation of aesthetic qualities in a two-dimensional visual language. The elements of this language—shapes, lines, colors, tones, and textures—are used to produce sensations of volume, space, movement, and light on a flat surface. Film

Film is a form of visual art used to imitate experiences and communicate ideas, stories, or feelings with moving images. The film industry in the Philippines began in 1897.
