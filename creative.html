Creative Writing


Literary Devices

1. Imagery

Imagery is a concrete representation of an idea to convey a message using language that evokes the five senses. There are five types of imagery:

  • Olfactory imagery (sense of smell)
  • Tactile imagery (sense of touch)
  • Visual imagery (sense of sight)
  • Auditory imagery (sense of hearing)
  • Gustatory imagery (sense of taste)

For example: "Stars that shine and twinkle on the milky way" evokes the sense of sight through the words stars, shine, and twinkle.

2. Figures of Speech

Figures of speech, also known as figurative language, create mental images for the readers. Examples include simile, metaphor, personification, hyperbole, and many more.

  • Hyperbole: Expressing ideas in an exaggerated manner.
    Example: "I almost turned crazy thinking about you."
  • Onomatopoeia: Using words that imitate sounds.
    Example: "I miss the splashing of the waters during summer time."
  • Personification: Assigning human qualities to inanimate objects.
    Example: "The sea is so angry over the streak of garbage at the bay."
  • Oxymoron: Joining two opposite ideas.
    Example: "The loud silence of the streets can be heard during the lockdown."
  • Rhetorical Question: A question asked for emphasis, not requiring an answer.
    Example: "Who does not want to be free?"

3. Diction

Diction refers to the choice of words in a literary work. It can be formal, informal, colloquial, or slang.

  • Formal: Uses third-person pronouns; contractions are not allowed.
    Example: "Frontliners selflessly serve during this pandemic."
  • Informal: Allows first-person pronouns and contractions.
    Example: "I want to shout out to my friends who passed the examination."
  • Colloquial: Uses casual words.
    Example: "They ain't got nothing to say."
  • Slang: Informal language used by specific groups.
    Example: "That performance was epic."

4. Specific Experiences

Identifying specific experiences in a literary work allows readers to relate to the writer. For example, a writer may find pleasure in nature, as expressed in the line: "A poet could not but be gay."
